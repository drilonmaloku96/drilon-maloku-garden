Recently, I came to the realization that anytime deliberate thinking is demanded of me, I tend to turn to AI to outsource the headache.

Want to reorganize the balcony? Take a picture of it, measure the dimensions in meters with an app, and then give the information in a usable way to the AI with a vague idea of the vibe I want it to radiate. It gave me concrete suggestions for improvement and even pointed out what I had done right so far with the balcony—and why it was right to have done so. The advice on furniture, arrangement, and aesthetics was shockingly good. But I must admit, at first, I was too lazy to even read the concrete answer. I wanted even less for my brain to compute. So, without giving the text a second glance, I asked for drawn sketches of how it should look. Once again, the sketches were impressively accurate.  

What finally made me pause, go back, and read the whole text carefully—actually burning the calories to visualize it for myself—was the moment I found myself already browsing Amazon for specific items to put in my cart. Then a thought crept from the back of my brain: Have I started living the unexamined life? Did I already concede, in such a short time, to my intellect being inferior to that of AI?

What stings the most is the fact that I didn’t even question the AI’s suggestions—it looked good enough that I saw no need to explore variations or alternatives. What depresses me even further is that even after deliberately analyzing what the AI suggested and mentally picking it apart, I couldn’t come up with any substantial improvement.

Want to reorganize the bookshelf? I did. I had taken a look at it the day before yesterday and had the hunch that it didn’t look beautiful. My hand twitched for my phone instinctively, and I gave in to the primitive urge to outsource my thinking. I took a picture of my bookshelf—but this time, I wanted to use the AI more mindfully. So, I asked for an evaluation of its look: what exactly is not satisfactory about the way it’s currently arranged, and how I might improve it.

It told me the overall vibe of the bookshelf was good—I had left small memorabilia and objects from my hobbies to give it enough ‘soul’ and a ‘vibe.’ (Thank God I didn’t embarrass myself in the eyes of the AI.) But the distribution of the objects was asymmetrical, which caused its figurative weight to be tilted to one side. This, the AI stated, gives a subjective impression of it not being pleasant to the eye. (What?!?)

I saw its point and got to work. I redistributed everything as it suggested: symmetrical, but not uniform. Some rows of books here, some horizontal stacks there—the centerpiece where the eye should focus… and… done! I looked at it. It looked very good. I understood the concepts the AI had talked about, and it was right.

But… since I always end up following the AI to the letter—why even bother reflecting on its suggestions?

Okay, for isolated things like this, the AI might be indispensable—but in real life, you have to be the central processing unit. Until… you don’t. At tricky problems where my wife and I are at a standstill and don’t know how to tackle something, we keep asking our good old friend ChatGPT what it thinks. It resolved many of the things we were racking our brains about, or at least gave us a framework for how to attack the issue more effectively.

It won’t be long—if it hasn’t happened already—before it becomes normal to include an AI guest in every discussion, consulted here and there during debate. (It’s already happened online, if you watched the Hasan Piker / Ethan Klein drama.) One step further would be the complete mediation of a social interaction between two humans through an AI agent. How would that even look?

I’m glad that my ChatGPT activity isn’t accessible to anyone but me. It’s easily one of the most private databases I have right now. I’ve babbled to it like an idiot, in incomplete, sometimes incoherent sentences, and let it answer—because I know how good it is at understanding me. I don’t even feel the need to phrase my requests grammatically correct, and it has made me significantly lazier than I used to be.

I’ve even started talking to my wife in incomplete, grammatically incorrect sentences—she has access to my most intimate thoughts, just like ChatGPT, so sometimes I forget that babbling like an idiot and expecting to be understood isn’t the norm in real-life interactions.

Where will this lead us?

Will we end up grunting incomprehensible, half-thought-through noises—and the AI will understand us well enough to decipher and fulfill our desires? Will we deteriorate in our ability to think and construct chains of reasoning since we keep delegating this resource-intensive task?

As a small act of rebellion, I’ve started writing more, to force myself to articulate thoughts. I’ve started reading more books, to challenge myself to extract ideas and understand overarching themes and meanings.

A paranoid thought lingers at the back of my mind: You write, but who will be the reader? Surely, even if published somewhere, it will be subject to an AI to be summarized for someone who doesn’t want to read the whole thing.

You think and rationalize and learn as you toil through a difficult book—but someone else will let the content be extracted and served to them on a golden platter, ready to be consumed. Same goal, vastly different energy consumption. Am I hemming my growth potential?

I should ask ChatGPT…
