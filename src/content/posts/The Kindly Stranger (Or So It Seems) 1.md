I skip on the bus with a jiggity-jog,
And find my four chairs—oh, what a hog!
Who saves them for me, with patience so sweet?
A foreigner! Always! Protecting my seat.

He waits there in silence, he waits there with care,
Ensuring my bottom has plenty of chair.
No elbows invade me, no knees near my knee,
It’s just me and him with our space, fancy-free.

How thoughtful he is! How polite and how grand,
As if he was born with a seat-saving hand.
He doesn’t need signs, he doesn’t need say,
The others just know to keep scooting away.

So every commute, with a smile ear to ear,
I thank all the strangers who save me my cheer.
…But then comes the truth, and it’s sharper than all:
The locals won’t sit where his shadow may fall.

So lucky for me, what a marvelous perk—
Their prejudice spares me the squeeze and the smirk.
